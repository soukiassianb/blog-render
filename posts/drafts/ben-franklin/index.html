Ben Franklin

I just got around finishing Benjamin Franklin’s biography by Walter Isaacson. I read it to understand why his character has been a source of fascination and admiration for many, and while the book was dense and at times a bit longish, by the end I definitely shared a form of admiration for the man (as I tend to do for all polymaths).

Here is a list of things I learned about Benjamin Franklin.

1. Printer

He started as a printer in Philadelphia and even though he had multiple lives, as a publisher, post master, scientist and politician, he kept referring to himself as a printer for all his life.

2. Publisher

Ben Franklin started working in his brother’s printing shop, which was at that time publishing a journal called the New English Courant. His brother didn’t want Franklin to publish in the journal so he slipped novels written under a pseudonym, “Mrs. Silence Dogwood” under the printing shop door. The novels became increasingly successful, forcing him to reveal his identity.

4. Runaway

At 17, he ran away from his brother’s shop and family with no money, traveling from Boston to New York then Philadelphia, where he found a job in another printing shop.

3. Self-improvement

Ben Franklin was the patron saint of self-improvement, spending time devising system of moral values and going so far as tracking his habits.

For instance, his “Plan for future conduct” included the following:

  1. It is necessary for me to be extremely frugal for some time, till I have paid what I owe.
  2. To endeavour to speak truth in every instance; to give nobody expectations that are not likely to be answered, but aim at sincerity in every word and action—the most amiable excellence in a rational being.
  3. To apply myself industriously to whatever business I take in hand, and not divert my mind from my business by any foolish project of growing suddenly rich; for industry and patience are the surest means of plenty.
  4. I resolve to speak ill of no man whatever, not even in a matter of truth; but rather by some means excuse the faults I hear charged upon others, and upon proper occasions speak all the good I know of every body.
